Tactile-LM Project

Does Affective Touch Matter for Development?
CT Afferent Modeling in the MIMo Infant Simulation

Youchan Yim
Institute of Systems and Information Engineering, University of Tsukuba
📄 Paper (PDF) Code (github) 🎬 Video

Abstract

Touch is the first sense to mature, yet developmental simulations reduce it to scalar force, ignoring the affective dimension mediated by C-tactile (CT) afferents. We ask whether this omission matters: does CT-level tactile modeling produce qualitatively different sensory information compared to force-only models? To investigate this, we introduce CT-Touch, a biologically grounded tactile module for the MIMo infant simulation platform that models four mechanoreceptor types (SA-I, FA-I, FA-II, CT) with body-site-specific distributions. We validate the module through sensor-level analysis under random motor babbling and reinforcement learning experiments on self-body exploration and reaching tasks. Our results show that CT afferents provide a qualitatively new velocity-tuned signal absent in force-only mode and respond exclusively on hairy skin consistent with neurophysiology. Under RL training, both conditions achieve comparable self-body task performance, but CT-equipped agents show a consistent 10-18% advantage in the reach task with half the touch activation, suggesting more efficient contact behavior.

Key Findings

✓ Verified

7-channel vs 3-channel output

CT-Touch decomposes contact forces into SA-I (12.7%), FA-I (17.4%), FA-II (6.2%), CT (0.01%), and normal force (63.8%) — information invisible to force-only models.

✓ Verified

CT responds only on hairy skin

CT afferent activation is zero on glabrous skin (palms, fingertips) and non-zero on hairy skin (forearms, trunk, face) — matching human neuroanatomy.

✓ Verified

Velocity-tuned inverted-U

CT firing rate peaks at ~3 cm/s stroking velocity, following the log-Gaussian model from Löken et al. (2009).

◇ Hypothesis

CT shapes developmental trajectories

We predict CT afferents will causally enhance self-exploratory behavior and cross-modal representation formation. RL experiments are ongoing.

CT-Touch Module

CT-Touch extends MIMo's tactile system from a single force-vector sensor to a biologically grounded 4-receptor model. Each sensor point outputs responses for SA-I (Merkel), FA-I (Meissner), FA-II (Pacinian), and CT afferents, with body-site-specific distributions distinguishing hairy and glabrous skin.

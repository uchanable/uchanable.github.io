Abstract

Touch is the first sense to mature, yet developmental simulations reduce it to scalar force, ignoring the affective dimension mediated by C-tactile (CT) afferents. We ask whether this omission matters: does CT-level tactile modeling produce qualitatively different sensory information compared to force-only models? To investigate this, we introduce CT-Touch, a biologically grounded tactile module for the MIMo infant simulation platform that models four mechanoreceptor types (SA-I, FA-I, FA-II, CT) with body-site-specific distributions. We validate the module through sensor-level analysis under random motor babbling and reinforcement learning experiments on self-body exploration and reaching tasks. Our results show that CT afferents provide a qualitatively new velocity-tuned signal absent in force-only mode and respond exclusively on hairy skin consistent with neurophysiology. Under RL training, both conditions achieve comparable self-body task performance, but CT-equipped agents show a consistent 10-18% advantage in the reach task with half the touch activation, suggesting more efficient contact behavior.
Update: 30-seed replication (n=30 × 4 time steps = 480 runs) confirms the reach-task advantage with extreme Bayesian evidence (BF10 = 453).

System Architecture

Architecture

Figure 1. CT-Touch system architecture. MuJoCo contact forces are processed through four mechanoreceptor models (SA-I, FA-I, FA-II, CT), producing a 7-channel output per sensor point.

CT-Touch extends MIMo's tactile system from a single force-vector sensor to a biologically grounded 4-receptor model. MuJoCo raw contact forces are transformed through computational models of four mechanoreceptor types, producing a 7-channel output: 3 original force channels plus SA-I (Merkel), FA-I (Meissner), FA-II (Pacinian), and CT afferent responses. Body-site-specific distributions distinguish hairy and glabrous skin regions.

Mechanoreceptor Response Models

CT-Touch models four mechanoreceptor types:

  • SA-I (Merkel): Sustained pressure response with compressive nonlinearity. Output: sign(F) · log(1 + |F|) applied per-axis to the 3D contact force vector, producing 3 channels. Models slow-adapting pressure encoding essential for perceiving object shape and weight.
  • FA-I (Meissner): Velocity-proportional response capturing dynamic changes in contact force. Output: min(v · 100, 10) where v is the estimated contact velocity (tangential force change × dt × 10). Models rapid adaptation critical for slip and texture detection during manipulation.
  • FA-II (Pacinian): Rate-of-force-change response sensitive to rapid transients. Output: min(|dFn/dt| · 0.01, 5.0) where Fn is the normal force magnitude. Models detection of sudden contact onset/offset.
  • CT (C-tactile): See detailed model below.

CT Afferent Model

Velocity-tuned affective touch channel found exclusively in hairy skin. The firing rate follows a log-Gaussian inverted-U with force-dependent gating:

r_CT(v, F) = r_max · exp(-(ln v - ln v*)² / 2σ²) · gate(F)

where v* = 0.03 m/s (3 cm/s optimal stroking velocity), r_max = 1.0 (normalized), σ = 0.7, and gate(F) = min(1, F/2) · exp(−F/10) ensures CT responds only to gentle forces. Minimum force threshold = 0.1 N. Based on Löken et al. (2009, Nature Neuroscience) and corroborated by rodent C-LTMR electrophysiology (Abraira & Ginty, 2013). This inverted-U captures the preference for gentle, slow stroking that humans rate as most pleasant.

Note: The original Löken model includes a temperature modulation term g(T) peaking at ~32°C. Since MuJoCo does not simulate thermal physics, we replace g(T) with a force-dependent gate that serves an analogous role of filtering non-gentle contacts.

CT afferent firing rate vs. stroking velocity, showing the inverted-U response peaking at ~3 cm/s.

CT Model: Theoretical vs. Implementation

The ICDL submission described the theoretical CT model using the original parameters from Löken et al. (2009). The implementation adapts these for the RL pipeline. Both share the same log-Gaussian structure; the differences are in parameterization:

The qualitative behavior is preserved: an inverted-U peaking at 3 cm/s gentle stroking, active only on hairy skin. The normalized output and tighter σ improve signal utility as an RL observation without changing the fundamental biological model.

Experiment 1: Sensor Validation

Under random motor babbling (DummyEnv, 1000 steps × 3 seeds), we validate that CT-Touch produces biologically consistent outputs and qualitatively richer information than force-only touch.

Detailed Interpretation

  • Normal force dominates (55.7%): The raw normal force component accounts for over half of total activation because it captures all contact events regardless of dynamics. The remaining 44.3% represents the added information from receptor-type decomposition that is invisible to force-only models.
  • SA-I and FA-I are equal (17.4% each): SA-I responds to sustained pressure while FA-I responds to velocity changes. Their equal contribution under random babbling reflects the mixture of static contacts (leaning, resting) and dynamic contacts (arm swings, collisions).
  • FA-II is lower (9.5%): The acceleration-based Pacinian channel responds primarily to vibration and rapid force changes. Random babbling produces fewer high-frequency vibration events than sustained or velocity-based contacts.
  • CT is 0.01%: This is not a limitation but a feature. CT afferents are tuned to gentle stroking at ~3 cm/s; random motor babbling produces rapid, forceful contacts that rarely match this optimal velocity. Under learned policies (see RL results below), CT activation increases substantially (36-40 vs 0.05), indicating that goal-directed behavior naturally engages CT-rich hairy-skin regions.
  • Head and upper body show highest activation in the heatmap. This is consistent with MIMo's sitting posture in DummyEnv: the head and torso make frequent contact with the floor/ground surface, and random arm movements reach the head and upper body most easily. Seed-to-seed variation reflects the stochastic nature of random babbling.
  • Hairy vs. glabrous distinction: CT activation occurs exclusively on red-labeled (hairy) body parts with zero on blue-labeled (glabrous) parts, confirming that the body-site mapping is correctly integrated into MIMo's contact pipeline end-to-end.

Modeling Limitations

  • Simplified receptor models: SA-I, FA-I, and FA-II use first-order approximations (log compression, velocity scaling, rate-of-force-change) rather than detailed biophysical models. FA-II in particular captures force transients rather than true vibration-frequency tuning (~250 Hz Pacinian response).
  • No thermal simulation: MuJoCo lacks thermal physics, so the CT temperature modulation term g(T) from Löken et al. is replaced by a force-dependent gate. This is a reasonable proxy for thermoneutral environments but cannot model temperature-dependent affective touch modulation.
  • Developmental maturation not applied: CT-Touch includes a developmental trajectory model (0–24 months) with myelination progression and receptor maturation schedules (τAβ, τCT), but all experiments reported here use adult-level responses. The maturation parameters are first approximations awaiting empirical calibration.
  • Single platform: All experiments use MIMo + MuJoCo. However, the receptor models process generic contact forces and are portable to other physics engines (Isaac Gym, PyBullet, etc.).

Reinforcement Learning

Setup

  • Algorithm: PPO, 10 seeds per condition, identical reward structure for CT OFF / CT ON
  • Self-body task: reward +500 for touching target body part
  • Reach task: reward based on finger-target distance
  • Training steps: 50K, 100K, 500K, 1M (4 checkpoints × 2 conditions × 10 seeds = 80 runs per task)
  • Hardware: Mac Studio M1 Max, 64 GB RAM
  • Developmental scaling: Disabled (adult-level receptor responses). CT-Touch includes a developmental maturation model (0–24 months) but all RL experiments use full adult sensitivity to isolate the effect of CT afferents without confounding age-related changes.
  • Temperature: Fixed at 32°C (optimal for CT). MuJoCo has no thermal simulation; the force-dependent gate substitutes for the temperature modulation term.

Post-submission Replication (n=30)

Why 30 seeds?

The original 10-seed experiment showed a promising CT advantage in the reach task (Hedges' g = 0.60, p = .21), but the sample was too small to draw firm conclusions. Post-hoc power analysis revealed that n = 10 provides only 8.8% power to detect the true effect, meaning the observed effect size was likely inflated by small-sample noise. We therefore expanded to 30 seeds per condition (4 checkpoints × 2 conditions × 30 seeds = 240 runs per task, 480 total).

Takeaway

The 10-seed “trend” is confirmed as a small but reliable effect (g ≈ 0.3) by three converging lines of evidence: meta-analysis across training horizons (p = .016), Bayesian model comparison (BF10 = 453, extreme), and a consistent 67% win rate. Individual pairwise tests remain non-significant because power is only 21% at this sample size — but the combined evidence leaves little doubt that CT afferents provide a systematic, task-specific advantage for goal-directed reaching.

Why is the effect task-specific?

The CT advantage is absent in self-body exploration (BF10 = 0.111, moderate evidence for H0). Two complementary hypotheses explain this dissociation:

Hypothesis A

Reward structure mismatch

The self-body task uses a binary reward (+500 for any contact with the target body part). This reward is orthogonal to contact quality — it cannot distinguish a slow, gentle touch from a fast collision. Even if CT provides richer velocity-tuned information, the reward signal has no gradient to exploit it. Under this view, the null result reflects a measurement limitation, not absence of CT utility.

Hypothesis B

CT benefits precision control

The reach task requires precise finger-to-target coordination where approach velocity and contact dynamics matter. CT afferents provide exactly this — a velocity-tuned signal that helps the policy learn efficient approach trajectories. Self-body exploration, by contrast, rewards any contact regardless of finesse, making CT’s additional information redundant. Under this view, CT is genuinely task-specific.

These hypotheses are not mutually exclusive. The binary reward likely masks any CT contribution in the self-body task, while the reach task’s distance-based reward creates a gradient that CT signals can exploit. A future experiment with velocity-sensitive self-touch rewards (e.g., reward proportional to gentle stroking at ~3 cm/s) would disambiguate the two: if CT then shows an advantage, Hypothesis A is confirmed; if not, Hypothesis B stands alone. A subsequent body-part contact pattern analysis (n = 30 seeds, 3 episodes × 1000 steps) confirms that CT ON agents do not preferentially explore hairy-skin regions. The hairy-skin contact ratio is virtually identical between conditions for both tasks: self-body (CT ON 0.600 ± 0.117 vs CT OFF 0.606 ± 0.142; p = .728) and reach (CT ON 0.992 vs CT OFF 0.995; p = .796). CT channel activation in hairy-skin regions was notably low (max: left lower leg = 0.111), indicating that under binary reward the CT signal is too weak to drive differential exploration. This further supports Hypothesis A (reward structure mismatch): the agent has no incentive to seek CT-optimal contact when any touch is equally rewarded.

Interpretation

  • Self-body task: No meaningful difference between CT ON and CT OFF (BF10 = 0.111, moderate evidence for H0), consistent with the 10-seed results. This is expected — self-body exploration rewards any contact with the target body part, regardless of touch quality.
  • Reach task: The 10-seed trend is confirmed at n=30. CT ON consistently outperforms CT OFF across all 4 training horizons (Δ = +5 to +12 reward). While individual time-step comparisons do not reach p < .05, the combined Bayes Factor of 453.3 provides extreme evidence for a systematic CT advantage. CT ON agents use ~50% less touch activation, confirming the more efficient contact behavior observed at n=10.
  • Task-specific dissociation: The contrasting results suggest CT afferents selectively benefit tasks requiring precise, goal-directed reaching rather than exploratory self-touch, consistent with the hypothesis that affective touch channels refine sensorimotor control.
  • Training overhead: CT ON adds 3–23% wall-clock time due to mechanoreceptor computation, which is modest given the richer sensory representation.

SOM + Hebbian Cross-Modal Architecture (Ongoing)

Motivation

The 30-seed PPO replication confirmed a small but reliable CT advantage in the reach task (BF10 = 453). However, the effect size remains modest (g ≈ 0.3), suggesting that PPO alone struggles to fully exploit the high-dimensional tactile space. We hypothesized that a body-topographic intermediate representation could bridge this gap: a Self-Organizing Map (SOM) that learns the spatial structure of tactile input before it reaches the RL policy.

This idea was proposed in the ICDL submission (Section III-E) as the SOM + Hebbian cross-modal architecture, but was not implemented at the time of submission — a point noted by Reviewer W4. We have since completed the implementation and are currently running the full factorial experiment.

Architecture

The SOM module is implemented as a Gym observation wrapper, requiring no modifications to the MIMo codebase. It consists of three components:

Discriminative SOM

Body-topographic map

13 bodies × 6 channels = 78-dimensional input. Learns a spatial map that preserves body topology, encoding the full discriminative tactile signal (SA-I, FA-I, FA-II, force).

Affective SOM

CT-specific map

13 bodies × 1 CT channel = 13-dimensional input. A dedicated map for the CT afferent signal, capturing velocity-tuned affective touch patterns across body regions.

Proprioceptive SOM

Joint-angle map

MIMo’s proprioceptive observation (joint angles + velocities). Provides the motor context that links tactile sensation to body posture, enabling coordinated sensorimotor representation.

Hebbian Binding

3-pair cross-modal network

Three fully-connected Hebbian links bind the SOMs: Disc↔Aff, Disc↔Proprio, Aff↔Proprio. Co-active neurons strengthen connections; weight decay prevents saturation. Cross-modal prediction accuracy: 96% in unit tests.

  • Critical period scheduler: SOM plasticity (learning rate + neighborhood radius) is high during early training and decays gradually, mimicking the developmental critical period for somatotopic map formation.
  • Intrinsic motivation: Curiosity-driven exploration via SOM prediction error + CT reward signal.
  • Overhead: 2.2 ms/step — negligible compared to MuJoCo simulation and PPO update costs.
  • Zero MIMo modification: The entire SOM pipeline lives in a Gym wrapper (som_wrapper.py); MIMo source code is untouched.

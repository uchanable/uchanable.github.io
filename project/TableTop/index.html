Abstract

CT-optimal touch activates C-tactile afferents through gentle stroking at 3 cm/s with 0.4 N contact force, promoting relaxation and stress reduction. Existing devices are either stationary (precise but laboratory-bound) or wearable (portable but limited in force control).

We propose a tabletop mobile robot that delivers CT-optimal touch to the forearm during desk work, combining a motorized parallel-linkage stroking module with real-time force sensing. A feedforward+PID controller uses iterative surface learning to compensate for forearm curvature. A within-subjects study with 20 participants compared FF+PID against PID-only across parallel and orthogonal stroking directions.

FF+PID significantly reduced force error, variability, and temporal fluctuations in both directions with large effect sizes. No differences were found in subjective evaluation, yet electrodermal activity analysis indicated reduced physiological arousal under FF+PID in the orthogonal direction.

System Overview

Force Metrics

FF+PID reduced RMSE by 18–28% and CV by 25–31% compared to PID-only, with large effect sizes ($|d| > 0.85$) in 11 of 12 comparisons.

Data processing pipeline

Force data extracted via three-stage filtering: stroke direction filter, moving-point filter, and steady-state filter. Only constant-velocity, steady-state samples during active stroking are analyzed.

Metric definitions

All metrics computed over steady-state data for each participant–condition pair (10 cycles pooled). Target force $F_{\text{target}} = 0.4$ N.

Mean

Global measure of how closely the controller maintains the target force on average.
$$\overline{F} = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^{n} F_i$$

RMSE

Magnitude of force deviation from the target. Squaring amplifies large errors, making it sensitive to transient force spikes.
$$\text{RMSE} = \sqrt{\frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^{n}(F_i - F_{\text{target}})^2}$$

MAE

Average absolute deviation from the target force. Less sensitive to outliers than RMSE; reported as a complementary accuracy metric.
$$\text{MAE} = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^{n}|F_i - F_{\text{target}}|$$

CV

Force variability normalized by the mean, enabling fair comparison across conditions with slightly different mean forces.
$$\text{CV} = \frac{\text{SD}(F)}{\overline{F}} \times 100$$

$\Delta F$ (Hysteresis)

Absolute difference in mean force between forward and backward strokes. Lower values indicate more consistent bidirectional force delivery.
$$\Delta F = |\overline{F}_\text{fwd} - \overline{F}_\text{bwd}|$$

MASD

Force smoothness: average absolute sample-to-sample changes within each cycle, then averaged across $K=10$ cycles. Lower MASD = smoother delivery.
$$\text{MASD} = \frac{1}{K}\sum_{k=1}^{K}\!\left(\frac{1}{n_k\!-\!1}\sum_{i=2}^{n_k}|F_i^{(k)}\!-\!F_{i-1}^{(k)}|\right)$$

Discussion

FF+PID consistently improved force control across both directions. The sole exception—orthogonal $\Delta F$—reflects the robot's kinematics (motor axis perpendicular to stroke), not a controller limitation.

MASD analysis

Orthogonal MASD is higher than parallel (0.019 vs 0.007) despite lower RMSE, due to structural differences: ~11 data points per cycle vs ~74, and ~2× longer sample interval. Cross-direction MASD comparison is inappropriate; only within-direction comparisons are meaningful.

Table S2. Sampling characteristics between parallel and orthogonal stroking.

Forward/backward separated analysis

Parallel: FF+PID significantly reduced hysteresis ($p < 0.001$, $d = -1.047$). Orthogonal: minimal hysteresis (~0.03 N) in both conditions. Combined RMSE inflation from hysteresis: max 5.3%. FF+PID superiority unchanged when strokes are analyzed separately.

Measurement Reliability

Variance decomposition

FF+PID: higher Between/Within SD ratios (6.66 vs 2.76 for parallel), confirming anatomy-driven variability rather than controller instability.

Table S3. Variance decomposition results.

Intraclass correlation (ICC)

All conditions: ICC 0.855–0.998 ("good" to "excellent"). FF+PID yielded higher ICC with non-overlapping 95% CIs.

Table S4. ICC results.

Cycle trend distribution

Improving 26%, Stable 44%, Worsening 30%—balanced distribution confirms no systematic learning or fatigue effects.

X-axis drift analysis

All 20 participants: systematic drift ~0.135 cm/cycle (friction-induced heading PID overcorrection). Independent of FF compensation; negligible impact (within-participant RMSE SD = 0.002 N, <0.5% of target).

Table S5. X-axis drift per participant.

Calibration BWD[0] analysis

BWD[0] angle correlates with forearm anatomy and parallel RMSE ($r = 0.606$, $p = 0.005$). Four outlier participants showed modestly higher RMSE but did not change the FF+PID superiority pattern. Identifies forearm anatomy as a system limitation addressable through adaptive calibration.

Interpretation

The orthogonal SCL finding connects to variance decomposition: orthogonal PID had the highest within-participant RMSE variability (SD = 0.010 N). While not consciously detected, the force inconsistency registered at an unconscious physiological level.

Calibration Algorithms

Two-phase calibration adapts $\boldsymbol{\theta}_{\text{FF}}(y)$ to each participant's forearm. $N = 5$ points, $K = 5$ trials, $\alpha = 1.0$, $F^* = 0.4$ N.

Algorithm S1 — Static surface scan

Measures motor angles at 5 points in both directions, then linearly interpolates.

Algorithm S2 — Dynamic learning + monotonicity filter

Update: $\theta^{(k)}(y_i) = \theta^{(k-1)}(y_i) + \alpha \cdot u_{\text{PID}}(y_i)$ when $|e| \geq 0.05$ N.

Algorithm S3 — Real-time FF+PID control

$\theta_{\text{cmd}} = \theta_{\text{FF}}(y) - u_{\text{PID}}$. Gains: $K_p = 0.3$, $K_i = K_d = 0.03$. 10 round trips per condition.

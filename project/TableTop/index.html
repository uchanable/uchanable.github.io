§Abstract

CT-optimal touch activates C-tactile afferents through gentle stroking at 3 cm/s with 0.4 N contact force, promoting relaxation and stress reduction. Existing devices are either stationary (precise but laboratory-bound) or wearable (portable but limited in force control). We propose a tabletop mobile robot (94×137×72 mm) that delivers CT-optimal touch to the forearm during desk work, combining a motorized parallel-linkage stroking module with real-time force sensing. A feedforward+PID (FF+PID) controller uses iterative surface learning to compensate for forearm curvature. A within-subjects study with 20 participants compared FF+PID against PID-only across parallel and orthogonal stroking directions. FF+PID significantly reduced force error, variability, and temporal fluctuations in both directions with large effect sizes, while all participants showed consistent improvements with high measurement reliability. No differences were found in subjective evaluation, yet electrodermal activity analysis indicated reduced physiological arousal under FF+PID in the orthogonal direction.

S1.1 Data Processing Pipeline

The force data were extracted from raw stroking recordings using a three-stage filtering pipeline. Each stroke cycle consists of: stop → acceleration → steady (analyzed) → deceleration → stop.

  1. Stroke direction filter: Turnaround segments where the robot is stationary (stroke = s) are removed.
  2. Moving-point filter: Data points where the Y-position is unchanged from adjacent samples are removed, eliminating boundary points that are still physically stationary.
  3. Steady-state filter: Within each continuous moving segment, the first and last data points are excluded to remove acceleration/deceleration transients.

The resulting data comprise only the constant-velocity, steady-state force samples during active stroking.

S1.2 Metric Definitions

All metrics are computed over the steady-state moving data for each participant–condition pair (10 cycles pooled). Target force: $F_{\text{target}} = 0.4$ N.

Mean

$$\overline{F} = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^{n} F_i$$

Global measure of how closely the controller maintains the target force on average.

RMSE

$$\text{RMSE} = \sqrt{\frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^{n}(F_i - F_{\text{target}})^2}$$

Magnitude of force deviation from the target. Sensitive to transient force spikes.

MAE

$$\text{MAE} = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^{n}|F_i - F_{\text{target}}|$$

Average absolute deviation from the target. Less sensitive to outliers than RMSE.

CV (Coefficient of Variation)

$$\text{CV} = \frac{\text{SD}(F)}{\overline{F}} \times 100$$

Force variability normalized by the mean, enabling fair cross-condition comparison.

$\Delta F$ (Forward–Backward Hysteresis)

$$\Delta F = |\overline{F}_{\text{forward}} - \overline{F}_{\text{backward}}|$$

Absolute difference in mean force between forward and backward strokes.

MASD (Mean Absolute Successive Difference)

$$\text{MASD} = \frac{1}{K}\sum_{k=1}^{K}\left(\frac{1}{n_k - 1}\sum_{i=2}^{n_k}|F_i^{(k)} - F_{i-1}^{(k)}|\right)$$

Force smoothness: average absolute sample-to-sample changes within each cycle, then averaged across $K = 10$ cycles. Lower MASD = smoother force delivery.

S1.4 Discussion

FF+PID consistently improved force control across both directions with large effect sizes ($|d| > 0.85$ for 11/12 comparisons). The sole exception—orthogonal $\Delta F$—reflects the robot's kinematics (motor axis perpendicular to stroke), not a controller limitation.

MASD Analysis

Orthogonal MASD values are substantially higher than parallel (FF+PID: 0.019 vs 0.007), despite lower RMSE. This is due to structural differences: orthogonal strokes have ~11 data points per cycle vs ~74 for parallel (shorter 2 cm distance), and the sample interval is ~2× longer (259 vs 133 ms). Cross-direction MASD comparison is therefore inappropriate; only within-direction FF+PID vs PID comparisons are meaningful.

Table S2. Sampling characteristics comparison between parallel and orthogonal stroking.

S1.5 Forward/Backward Separated Analysis

In parallel: FF+PID significantly reduced hysteresis ($p < 0.001$, $d = -1.047$), correcting directional force asymmetry from friction/inertia/backlash. In orthogonal: both conditions already had minimal hysteresis (~0.03 N) due to perpendicular motor axis. Combined RMSE inflation from hysteresis: max 5.3%. FF+PID superiority is unchanged when strokes are analyzed separately.

S2.Measurement Reliability

Three complementary analyses evaluate the reliability and stability of the 10-cycle repeated RMSE measurements.

S2.1 Variance Decomposition

Separates total RMSE variability into between-participant and within-participant components. Higher Between/Within ratio indicates inter-individual variation (anatomy) dominates over controller instability.

Table S3. Variance decomposition. FF+PID: higher Between/Within ratios (6.66 vs 2.76 for parallel), confirming anatomy-driven variability rather than controller instability.

S2.2 Intraclass Correlation Coefficients (ICC)

ICC(3,$k$): two-way mixed model, consistency agreement, average measures.

Table S4. All conditions achieved ICC 0.855–0.998 ("good" to "excellent"). FF+PID yielded higher ICC in both directions, with non-overlapping 95% CIs confirming statistically significant reliability improvement.

S2.3 Cycle Trend Distribution

Spearman $\rho$ between cycle number (1–10) and per-cycle RMSE for each of 80 participant–condition pairs. Balanced distribution confirms no systematic learning or fatigue effects.

Figure S3. Distribution of Spearman $\rho$ values. Improving: 21 (26%), Stable: 35 (44%), Worsening: 24 (30%). Balanced distribution confirms no systematic bias.

Parallel FF+PID Worsening

Parallel FF+PID showed a concentrated worsening pattern (14/20, 70%, Wilcoxon $p = 0.0002$). However, the Within-P SD is only 0.002 N (0.5% of target force)—a floor-effect regime where the already-optimal controller exhibits minute RMSE increases.

S2.4 X-Axis Drift Analysis

All 20 participants showed systematic negative X-axis drift (~0.135 cm/cycle), from $X \approx 0$ (cycle 1) to $X \approx -1.22$ cm (cycle 10).

Table S5. X-axis drift analysis: per-participant data, trend groups, and cycle-by-cycle grand average.

Mechanism: Heading PID Overcorrection

Contact friction creates lateral force → IMU interprets as heading perturbation → PID overcorrects asymmetrically between forward/backward strokes (|BWD|/|FWD| ratio ≈ 3). The drift is independent of FF compensation (FF+PID and PID produce nearly identical drift) and has negligible impact (within-participant RMSE SD = 0.002 N).

S2.5 Calibration BWD[0] Analysis

BWD[0] turnaround angle (mean 58.6°, range 29.5°–110.0°) correlates with forearm anatomy. FF+PID showed significant correlation with parallel RMSE ($r = 0.606$, $p = 0.005$): extreme forearm anatomy produces less accurate FF calibration profiles.

Four outlier participants (S12, S14, S16, S18) had modestly higher RMSE but remained within the overall distribution. This identifies forearm anatomy as a system limitation addressable through adaptive calibration.

S3.Subjective Evaluation

After each condition, participants rated three 7-point Likert scales: Comfort (kaiteki), Naturalness (shizen), and Consistency (ittei).

Table S6. No significant differences for any scale (all $p > 0.05$). Orthogonal consistency: marginal trend ($p = 0.081$, $r = 0.39$).

Figure S4. Perceptual questionnaire responses. Blue = FF+PID, orange = PID-only. Responses clustered around 4–6 with no substantial separation.

Key finding: FF compensation improves objective force performance without degrading the subjective tactile experience. The sub-hundred millinewton-level control differences fall below the human tactile detection threshold.

S4.Electrodermal Activity (EDA)

S4.1 Measurement Setup & Signal Processing

BITalino (r)evolution board at 10 Hz. Ag/AgCl electrodes on left index and middle fingertips. 5-minute stabilization period.

  • SCL (Tonic): Raw conductance; metrics: Mean, SD, Range, AUC ($\mu$S·s via trapezoidal integration)
  • SCR (Phasic): 2nd-order Butterworth bandpass (0.05–3.0 Hz, zero-phase); metrics: Amplitude, Count, AUC (baseline-corrected, 5 s pre-stroking)

S18 excluded ($n = 19$): near-zero SCL (0.47–0.65 $\mu$S vs group ~9–10 $\mu$S).

S4.2 Results

Parallel: No significant differences (all $p > 0.4$, $|d| < 0.2$).

Orthogonal: All four SCL metrics significantly lower under FF+PID ($p < 0.05$, $|d| = 0.636$–$0.787$) → reduced physiological arousal.

S4.3 Interpretation

The orthogonal SCL finding links to variance decomposition: orthogonal PID had the highest within-participant RMSE variability (SD = 0.010 N), meaning cycle-to-cycle force fluctuations were most pronounced. While not consciously detected (Section S3), the force inconsistency was registered at an unconscious physiological level—elevated tonic arousal without conscious awareness.

S5.Statistical Power Analysis

Post-hoc power analysis using: $\text{Power} = \Phi\!\left(|d|\sqrt{n} - z_{\alpha/2}\right)$ where $z_{\alpha/2} = 1.96$ ($\alpha = 0.05$, two-tailed).

Table S9. Power analysis for force metrics. All significant results: power > 0.85. Minimum $n$ for 80% power: 2–18.

Summary by Domain
  • Force: All significant results achieved power > 0.96. $n_{\min}$: 3–18.
  • Questionnaire: Small effect sizes ($r < 0.4$). Non-significance = genuinely minimal differences, not insufficient power.
  • EDA: Significant orthogonal SCL: power ≥ 0.75 ($n = 19$). SCL Mean and AUC exceeded 0.80.
  • Overall: With $n = 20$, minimum detectable effect = $d = 0.626$. All significant effects exceeded $|d| > 0.67$.

S6.Calibration Algorithm Details

Two-phase per-participant calibration adapts a feedforward angle map $\boldsymbol{\theta}_{\mathrm{FF}}(y)$ to each participant's forearm geometry. Key parameters: $N = 5$ measurement points, $K = 5$ learning trials, $\alpha = 1.0$, $F^* = 0.4$ N.

Algorithm S1: Phase 1 — Static Surface Scan

Measures motor angles at 5 points in both forward (unloading) and backward (loading) directions, then linearly interpolates to form the initial angle maps.

Algorithm S2: Phase 2 — Dynamic Learning + Monotonicity Filter

Update rule: $\theta^{(k)}(y_i) = \theta^{(k-1)}(y_i) + \alpha \cdot u_{\text{PID}}(y_i)$ when $|e| \geq \delta_{\text{dz}}$ (0.05 N). 5 trials refine the map.

Ensures physically consistent (non-increasing) angle values by ceiling and floor checks along the stroke path.

Algorithm S3: Real-Time FF+PID Control

$\theta_{\text{cmd}} = \theta_{\text{FF}}(y) - u_{\text{PID}}$. PID gains: $K_p = 0.3$, $K_i = 0.03$, $K_d = 0.03$. Force: 20 ms sampling (5-pt moving avg). PID: 50 ms update. Logging: 100 ms. 10 round-trip cycles per condition.

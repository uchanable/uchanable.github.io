Abstract

CT-optimal touch activates C-tactile afferents through gentle stroking at 3 cm/s with 0.4 N contact force, promoting relaxation and stress reduction. Existing devices are either stationary (precise but laboratory-bound) or wearable (portable but limited in force control).

We propose a tabletop mobile robot that delivers CT-optimal touch to the forearm during desk work, combining a motorized parallel-linkage stroking module with real-time force sensing. A feedforward+PID controller uses iterative surface learning to compensate for forearm curvature. A within-subjects study with 20 participants compared FF+PID against PID-only across parallel and orthogonal stroking directions.

FF+PID significantly reduced force error, variability, and temporal fluctuations in both directions with large effect sizes. No differences were found in subjective evaluation, yet electrodermal activity analysis indicated reduced physiological arousal under FF+PID in the orthogonal direction.

Calibration Algorithms

Two-phase per-participant calibration adapts $\boldsymbol{\theta}_{\text{FF}}(y)$ to each participant's forearm geometry. Key parameters: $N = 5$ measurement points, $K = 5$ learning trials, $\alpha = 1.0$, $F^* = 0.4$ N.

Phase 1 — Static Surface Scan. Measures motor angles at 5 points in both forward (unloading) and backward (loading) directions, then linearly interpolates to form initial angle maps.

Experiment

Experiment Session

20 participants performed a within-subjects experiment (2 directions × 2 controllers = 4 conditions, counterbalanced). Each condition consisted of 10 round-trip stroking cycles at 3 cm/s with a target force of 0.4 N.

Data Processing Pipeline

Three-stage filtering

The force data were extracted from raw stroking recordings using a three-stage filtering pipeline. Each stroke cycle consists of: stop → acceleration → steady (analyzed) → deceleration → stop.

  1. Stroke direction filter (stroke ∈ {f, b}): Turnaround segments where the robot is stationary are removed, as static periods are irrelevant to force control evaluation during active stroking.
  2. Moving-point filter (label_moving = 1): Data points where the Y-position is unchanged from adjacent samples are removed. These represent boundary points that are labeled as forward/backward but are still physically stationary.
  3. Steady-state filter (label_steady = 1): Within each continuous moving segment, the first and last data points are excluded to remove acceleration and deceleration transients (one point each).

The resulting data comprise only the constant-velocity, steady-state force samples during active stroking. This pipeline is identical to the pilot study and is implemented in parse_raw_to_master.py.

Evaluation Method

Force metric definitions

All metrics computed over steady-state data for each participant–condition pair (10 cycles pooled). Target force $F_{\text{target}} = 0.4$ N.

Mean

Global measure of how closely the controller maintains the target force on average.
$$\frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^{n} F_i$$

RMSE

Magnitude of force deviation from the target. Squaring amplifies large errors, making it sensitive to transient force spikes.
$$\sqrt{\frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^{n}(F_i - F_{\text{target}})^2}$$

MAE

Average absolute deviation from the target force. Less sensitive to outliers than RMSE; reported as a complementary accuracy metric.
$$\frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^{n}|F_i - F_{\text{target}}|$$

CV

Force variability normalized by the mean, enabling fair comparison across conditions with slightly different mean forces.
$$\frac{\text{SD}(F)}{\overline{F}} \times 100$$

$\Delta F$ (Hysteresis)

Absolute difference in mean force between forward and backward strokes. Lower values indicate more consistent bidirectional force delivery.
$$|\overline{F}_\text{fwd} - \overline{F}_\text{bwd}|$$

MASD

Force smoothness: average absolute sample-to-sample changes within each cycle, then averaged across $K=10$ cycles. Lower MASD = smoother delivery.
$$\frac{1}{K}\sum_{k=1}^{K}\!\left(\frac{1}{n_k\!-\!1}\sum_{i=2}^{n_k}|F_i^{(k)}\!-\!F_{i-1}^{(k)}|\right)$$
Perceptual questionnaire

After each 10-cycle condition, participants rated three 7-point Likert scales (1 = not at all, 7 = very much):

  • Comfort (kaiteki): "Was the stroking touch comfortable?"
  • Naturalness (shizen): "Was the stroking touch natural?"
  • Consistency (ittei): "Was the stroking force consistent?"

As Likert-scale data are ordinal, the Wilcoxon signed-rank test was used for paired comparisons (FF+PID vs PID-only) within each direction. Effect size: $r = Z / \sqrt{N}$.

EDA measurement

BITalino (r)evolution board at 10 Hz. Ag/AgCl electrodes on left index and middle fingertips. 5-minute stabilization period. S18 excluded ($n = 19$) due to near-zero SCL.

SCL (Tonic)

Raw conductance values used directly. Metrics: Mean, SD, Range, AUC ($\mu$S·s via trapezoidal integration).

SCR (Phasic)

2nd-order Butterworth bandpass (0.05–3.0 Hz, zero-phase). Metrics: Amplitude, Count, AUC (baseline-corrected, 5 s pre-stroking).

Results

  • Values are M ± SD.
  • $t$ = paired $t$-test; $W$ = Wilcoxon signed-rank (Shapiro–Wilk, $\alpha = .05$).

Parallel

FF+PID was significantly superior across all six metrics with large effect sizes ($|d| > 0.85$). CV ($d = -2.476$) and RMSE ($d = -1.749$) showed the largest effects.

Orthogonal

FF+PID was significantly superior for five of six metrics. $\Delta F$ was the sole exception ($p = 0.933$): both conditions exhibited minimal hysteresis (~0.03 N) because the motor axis is perpendicular to the stroking direction.

Discussion

Overall

FF+PID consistently improved force control across both directions ($|d| > 0.85$ for 11/12 comparisons). The consistency across two mechanically distinct directions supports generalizability.

MASD analysis

Orthogonal MASD is higher than parallel (0.019 vs 0.007) despite lower RMSE, due to ~11 data points per cycle vs ~74. Cross-direction comparison is inappropriate; only within-direction comparisons are meaningful.
